
===========================================================================
   CROSS-MODEL ANALYSIS — Report-Ready Text
===========================================================================

1. ARCHITECTURAL COMPARISON
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
BERT is an encoder-only Transformer that uses BIDIRECTIONAL self-attention,
meaning each token can attend to all other tokens in the sequence. This
makes BERT excellent at understanding tasks (classification, NER) but
fundamentally incapable of left-to-right generation.

GPT (DistilGPT-2) is a decoder-only Transformer with MASKED (causal)
self-attention — each token can only attend to previous tokens. This
constraint enables generation by autoregressive token-by-token sampling.

Text-GAN uses a fundamentally different paradigm: two competing networks
(Generator: GRU + linear, Discriminator: 1D-CNN with multi-kernel filters)
trained adversarially. It does not use the Transformer architecture and
starts from scratch with a custom vocabulary.

2. TRAINING PARADIGM DIFFERENCES
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
BERT uses SUPERVISED fine-tuning: each training example has an explicit
label. The model learns by minimizing classification loss against ground
truth. Stable, well-understood, and produced the best results in this study
(Macro-F1 = 0.8384).

GPT uses SELF-SUPERVISED fine-tuning: the data is its own label. The model
learns to predict each next token from previous ones. Also stable, with
monotonically decreasing loss. Achieved a 4.70x perplexity reduction
from baseline (133.0 -> 28.3).

Text-GAN uses ADVERSARIAL training: the Generator and Discriminator have
competing objectives. This is fundamentally less stable. In our experiment,
the Discriminator dominated the Generator after epoch 4, achieving 100%
accuracy and depriving G of useful gradient signal — a textbook failure
mode known as 'Discriminator dominance' that often co-occurs with 'mode
collapse' in the Generator's outputs.

3. PERFORMANCE LIMITS OBSERVED
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
BERT: Per-class F1 reveals the model is weakest on the smallest class.
Even with oversampling, the model's Positive recall (0.77) trails Negative
recall (0.90) and Neutral recall (0.88). This suggests Positive sentiment
is the harder signal to learn in financial news — possibly because positive
language often overlaps with neutral reporting.

GPT: Mild overfitting appeared after epoch 4 (validation loss began rising
from 3.343 to 3.352 while training loss continued falling). The best-model
auto-loading mechanism correctly preserved the epoch-4 checkpoint. The
model also hallucinates specific entities (fictional CEO names, fabricated
company combinations) — a known limitation of small-corpus generation.

Text-GAN: Collapsed into Discriminator dominance starting epoch 4. By
epoch 30, D total accuracy was 0.9999 (essentially perfect),
while G_loss climbed from 1.47 to 7.69, indicating G could not
find a path to improve. Final BLEU-2 = 0.0900, BLEU-4 = 0.0023.

4. BEHAVIORAL DIFFERENCES
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
BERT produces a structured 3-class probability distribution per input.
It cannot generate text. It is interpretable: per-class confidence is
directly readable.

GPT produces coherent, grammatically correct continuations with authentic
financial vocabulary (EUR, profit, net sales, operating period). It can
be conditioned on any prompt for controlled generation, but cannot
classify or evaluate sentiment.

Text-GAN, when trained successfully, would generate unconditional samples
from noise without any prompt. In our experiment, the collapsed Generator
produced repetitive, low-coherence sequences dominated by rare tokens —
the visible signature of mode collapse.

5. CONCLUSION
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
This comparative study confirms that the three NLP paradigms — discriminative
representation (BERT), generative autoregression (GPT), and adversarial
generation (GAN) — have meaningfully different stability profiles, output
behaviors, and resource requirements. For domain-specific NLP tasks with
limited labeled data, supervised fine-tuning of pretrained encoders (BERT)
provides the most reliable performance. Self-supervised fine-tuning of
pretrained decoders (GPT) is the natural choice for controlled text
generation. Adversarial training for text remains technically challenging
on small corpora and requires careful balancing — failures like the one
observed here are documented and expected in the literature.
